Career Builder is designed as an interactive guide to help you navigate the career planning process and find the most appropriate information resources whatever stage you're at. To get the most from the Career Builder it should be used in conjunction with other LSE Careers services and resources.

  1. Self assessment
    What are my skills and values?

    The first step in career planning is usually self-assessment. The better you know yourself, the easier it will be to find a fulfilling career.

    Self-assessment activities can include reflection, asking for feedback from people you know well, completing personality assessment tools or making lists that can bring to light important information about your skills, abilities, values and motivations.

    Some people find self-reflection easy whereas others find it more difficult. The activities below should help you get started.

    • Skills and abilities

      Pick the skills and abilities which best describe you. Try to focus on just a few. You should be able to describe in detail how you have demonstrated each skill.

      If you're finding this difficult then think about what your friends and family would say were your greatest strengths.

      Pick your skills

      • Team work

        Team work involves working confidently within a group, contributing your own ideas, taking a share of the responsibility, being assertive rather than passive or aggressive, accepting constructive criticism and being able to deliver constructive criticism to others.

      • Spoken communication

        Effective spoken communication requires being able to express your ideas and views clearly, confidently and concisely in speech, tailoring your content and style to the audience and facilitating communication amongst others.

      • Written communication

        Written communication involves expressing yourself clearly, using language with precision, constructing a logical argument, note taking, editing and summarising, and writing reports and essays.

      • Leadership

        Leadership involves being able to motivate and inspire others whilst taking responsibility for the direction and action of the team.

      • Problem solving

        Problem solving involves the ability to understand a problem by breaking it down into smaller parts, and identifying the key issues, implications and solutions.

      • Time management

        Time management involves prioritising, organising your work schedule, list making, persevering to get a task done and avoiding procrastination.

      • Commercial awareness

        Commercial awareness involves an interest in business and an understanding of the wider environment in which an organisation operates. It also includes knowledge of how businesses make money, what customers want, and what problems there are in particular areas of business.

      • Researching

        Researching involves searching carefully, with a method, so that you can answer a question. It is wider than finding out a fact and more focused than reading widely around a subject.

      • Decision making

        Decision making is the act of choosing between two or more actions. It involves the ability to weigh up different options, identify the pros and cons, and then take a decision based on the information available at the time.

      • Numeracy

        Numeracy involves the ability to use data and mathematics to support evidence or demonstrate a point.

      • Taking initiative

        Initiative involves having new ideas which can be made into a reality, including showing a strong personal drive and not waiting to be told to do things.

      • Flexibility

        Flexibility involves adapting successfully to changing situations and environments and thinking quickly to respond to sudden changes in circumstances.

  2. Careers research
    What kinds of careers are out there?

    Careers research can be broken down into three areas:

    • understanding of job sectors/industries
    • understanding of roles
    • understanding of organisations/employers

    Once you have completed research into these areas you will be better prepared to create tailored applications and find roles which match your skills and values. You may need to make small compromises when initially embarking on a career.

  3. Making a decision
    What career options are best for me?

    Decision making plays a key role in career planning and having too many choices will confuse you and slow you down. We all approach decision making in different ways. Some people favour following their instincts, some take a 'pot luck' approach, and a fortunate few are lucky enough to have always known what they want to do!

    In our experience, action occurs quickest when people recognise barriers to decision making which can often result from their own attitude rather than external factors.

    • Do I need to use this section?

      Lots of people find it easy to make decisions. From your research and reflection in sections 1 and 2 you might already have a really clear idea of what role, industry or employer you want to focus on.

      If you're not finding decision making difficult then you can skip this section. However you might still find the further support and advice useful.

      If you are finding it difficult to assess your options then continue to the barriers to decision making section below.

    • Barriers to effective decision making

      The following exercise will help you to reflect on what may be preventing you moving forward with your career decision making. The statements below are commonly cited reasons why LSE students find it difficult to take the next step. This isn't an exhaustive list so please come and talk to us if there are other reasons making it difficult for you to plan your career.

      Common barriers to career decision making

    • Reviewing your responses

      Looking for the perfect job? There are many jobs that will suit you.

      Remember that there isn't a single perfect career for you. There are usually a range of possibilities that would suit you. Looking for the perfect job can actually prevent you from trialling some really exciting opportunities. The perfect job rarely exists and your preferences and the nature of the job are likely to change over time. Don't make the mistake of passing up great work experience opportunities if the role does not match all of your criteria. You will find you have to compromise on some things; choosing a career is not an exact science.

      If you are interested in working abroad you should view the international careers section of our website.

      Trialling career options now will help you in the future

      Remember that the career choice you make now is not for life! Most LSE graduates will make several career changes throughout their working lives. If the first role you do does not work out, then you will have a better understanding of what is important to you when you make the next choice. Focus on a few ideas at a time.

      Remember that you can always review and adapt your plans and goals as they develop and take shape. Why not use your time at university to try out a variety of roles so you can get a flavour of the work involved? You can find a list of work placements, part time work and internships currently advertised on CareerHub.

      Compromise is important in effective decision making

      If one particular factor, like job location or using languages, has a major priority in your choice of career, you may have to compromise on some other factors to help you get started. For example, you may have to take a job which is not in your chosen sector, in order to make use of your languages in the first instance.

      Alternatively, in the short term you may have to compromise on your main deciding factor in career choice to enable you to realise your ambition longer term. For example, if your main priority is to live in London, but you also want to work in international development, you may have to gain experience in the field first, to enable you to move back to London at a later stage.

      If you are interested in working abroad you should view the international careers section of our website.

      Happiness and success in a career revolve around your choices

      If you are concerned about what other people think then you need to assess how important these influences are for you. Is it important for you to work in a similar sector to your friends?

      In career discussions we commonly hear students admitting to applying for certain sectors or roles as many of their friends or class mates were also applying. You have to be genuinely interested in a role to actually enjoy the work. Happiness and success in your career is personal and not related to what your friends are doing.

      More significant can be the influences of parents or partners. You need to be honest with yourself and with them about how important their views are.

      It's important to take control of your situation

      It often feels easier to put off making the big decisions. Even if you are planning on looking for a job following your exams there are still small things you can be doing whilst at university to make the process easier when you finish. For example you could try out a role of interest by arranging a week's work shadowing or you could take up a position of responsibility within a society or club. This will help you to identify areas of career interest and develop your skills now.

      If you plan to travel make sure you know what your first steps will be when you return. The sooner you start thinking about it, the more likely you'll be able to avoid being overwhelmed with career thoughts after your trip.

      If you are interested in working abroad you should view the international careers section of our website.

      Build your confidence - you have a lot to offer an employer!

      If you lack confidence in yourself, try to be realistic. Remember you will graduate from a globally respected university with a range of transferable academic skills and personal qualities. Review the skills audit from section 1 - Self assessment and refer back to your Personal Development Aide Memoire to gain a clearer understanding of what you have already engaged in.

      Voluntary work is valuable as it can help you to develop skills and may even help you identify a career path. Most careers can be achieved through gaining a range of work experience - it's about finding the right experience to get you there!

      If you feel confidence issues are preventing you from moving forward, the student counselling service can support you with any personal factors which might be affecting your career decisions.

      Careers support for students with disabilities

      If you feel a disability is preventing you from moving forward, please view our students with disabilities site or arrange an appointment with Viki Chinn, our specialist careers consultant for students with disabilities. Email careers.disability@lse.ac.uk or contact LSE Careers to make an appointment.
