• Who are you?1

    The first step to career planning is self-assessment. The better you know yourself, the easier it will be to find a fulfilling career. Self-assessment activities can include reflection, asking for feedback from people you know well, completing personality assessment tools or making 'inventories' that can bring to light important information about your skills, abilities, values and motivations.

    Some people find self-reflection easy whereas others find it more difficult. The activities below should help you get started. If you are struggling to identify your skills and values come and meet with one of our careers consultants for a careers discussion.

    • Skills and abilities

      Pick the skills and abilities which best describe you.

      If you're finding this difficult then think about what your friends and family would say were your greatest strengths.

      Team work
      Teamwork involves working confidently within a group, contributing your own ideas, taking a share of the responsibility, being assertive rather than passive or aggressive, accepting constructive criticism and being able to deliver constructive criticism to others.
      Spoken communication
      Effective spoken communication requires being able to express your ideas and views clearly, confidently and concisely in speech, tailoring your content and style to the audience and promoting free-flowing communication.
      Written communication
      Written Communication involves expressing yourself clearly, using language with precision; constructing a logical argument; note taking, editing and summarising; and writing reports.
      Leadership
      Leadership involves being able to motivate and inspire others whilst taking responsibility for the direction and action of the team.
      Problem solving
      Problem solving involves the ability to understand a problem by breaking it down into smaller parts, and identifying the key issues, implications and identifying solutions. To apply your knowledge from many different areas to solving a task.
      Time management
      Time management involves prioritising, organising your work schedule, list making, persevering to get a task done and avoiding procrastination.
      Commercial awareness
      Commercial awareness involves understanding the economics of the business, the business benefits and commercial realities from both the organisation's and the customer's perspectives.
      Researching
      Researching involves searching carefully, with a method, so that you can answer a question. It is wider than finding out a fact and more focused than reading widely around a subject.
      Decision making
      Decision making is the act of choosing between two or more courses of action. It involves the ability to weigh up different options, identify the pros and cons then take a decision based on the information available at the time.
      Flexibility
      Flexibility involves adapting successfully to changing situations and environments and thinking quickly respond to sudden changes in circumstances.
      Creativity
      Creativity involves using your inspiration and imagination to solve problems by looking at them from unexpected perspectives. Includes the Ability to generate and recognise how best practice and imaginative ideas can be applied to different situation
      Numeracy
      Numeracy involves the ability to use data and mathematics to support evidence or demonstrate a point.
      Taking initiative
      Initiative involves having new ideas which can be made into a reality. Showing a strong personal drive and not waiting to be told to do things.

      • Making a decision3

        Decision making plays a key role in career planning and having too many choices will confuse you and slow you down. We all approach decision making in different ways. Some favour following their instincts, some take a pot luck approach whilst a fortunate few are lucky enough to have always known what they want to do!

        In our experience, action occurs quickest when people recognise their decision making barriers which can often result from their own attitude rather than external factors.

        • Reviewing your responses

          Please select one or more decision making barriers from the section above.

          Looking for the perfect job? This may not actually exist!

            Remember that there isn't a single perfect career for you. There are usually a range of possibilities that would suit you. Looking for the perfect job can actually prevent you from trialing some really exciting opportunities. The perfect job rarely exists and your preferences and the nature of the job are likely to change over time. Don't make the mistake of passing up great work experience opportunities as the role does not match every marker. You will find you have to compromise on some things; this is not an exact science.

            Afraid of making the wrong decision? Trial it – you've got nothing to lose

              Remember, the career choice you make now is not a life sentence! Most LSE graduates will make several career changes throughout their working lives. If the first role you do does not work out, then you will have a better understanding of what is important to you when you make the next choice. Focus on a few ideas at a time. Remember that you can always review and adapt your plans and goals as they develop and take shape. Why not use your time at university to try out a variety of roles so you can get a flavour of the work involved?

              Compromise may be the key

                If one particular factor, like location or using languages, has a major priority in your choice of career, you may have to compromise on some other factors to help you get started. For example, you may have to take a job which is not in your chosen field, in order to make use of your languages in the first instance. Alternatively, in the short term you may have to compromise on your main deciding factor in career choice to enable you to realise your ambition longer term. For example, if your main priority is to live in London, but you also want to work in international development, you may have to gain experience in the field first, to enable you to move back to London at a later stage.

                Worried about what people will think?

                  If you are concerned about what other people think then you need to assess how important these influences are for you. Is it that important for you to work in a similar sector to your friends? In career discussions we commonly hear students admitting to applying for a role as many of their friends were also applying. You have to be genuinely interested in a role to actually enjoy the work so does it really matter what your LSE friends are doing? More significant can be the influences of parents or partners – you need to be honest with yourself and with them about how important their views are.

                  Are you opting out?

                    It often feels easier to put off making the big decisions. Even if you are planning on looking for a job following your exams there are still small things you can be doing whilst at university to make the process easier when you finish. If you plan to travel – make sure you know what your first steps will be when you return. In reality, very few people find 'themselves' or their ideal career whilst daydreaming on a beach!

                    Do you lack confidence?

                      If you lack confidence in yourself, try to be realistic. Remember you will graduate from a globally respected university with a range of transferable academic skills and personal qualities. Review your PDAM to gain a clearer understanding of what you have already engaged in. Most careers can be achieved through gaining a range of work experience – it's about finding the right experience to get you there!
